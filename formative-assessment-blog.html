Making Learning Visible: How I Use Formative Assessment in My Classroom

Gracielle Oliveira Mota Dias — IB Computer Science & Design Innovation Teacher, Pan American School of Bahia

Formative assessment has become one of the most powerful parts of my teaching practice. I see it not only as a way to check understanding but also as a tool to make thinking visible—for both students and for me as their teacher. It transforms the classroom into a space where learning is dynamic, collaborative, and full of discovery.

The “Sandwich Algorithm”

To explore algorithmic thinking, I divided students into two groups and gave them real sandwich ingredients. Each group had to write step-by-step instructions for assembling a sandwich, as if they were writing a program. Then, the groups swapped “code” and followed each other’s instructions exactly.

The results were hilarious—and insightful. Students quickly realized that missing a single step or misusing a word could completely alter the outcome. This simple exercise made the logic of algorithms tangible and fun.

What worked: Students were highly engaged, creative, and collaborative.
Challenge: Some needed extra guidance turning natural language into structured steps.

The “Real-Life Maze Challenge”

Another formative activity I love is the “Real-Life Maze Challenge.” I built a physical maze in the classroom using tape and desks, dividing students into teams with roles: Programmer, Debugger, and Executor. Together, they had to create and test a sequence of commands to navigate the maze successfully.

Watching them test, fail, and debug was incredible. The laughter and focus filled the room, and behind it all was deep learning about iteration, collaboration, and perseverance.

What worked: The activity built teamwork, logical reasoning, and resilience.
Challenge: It required time and flexibility to manage different group paces.

Reflection: What Worked and What Was Challenging

Both activities turned abstract ideas into experiences. Students didn’t just learn algorithms—they felt them. The simplicity, humor, and collaboration made the learning process stick.

Of course, these lessons also required careful setup and timing. But that’s what makes them powerful: they show that learning takes planning, flexibility, and reflection from both teacher and students.

Final Reflection

Formative assessment, to me, is a living dialogue. It celebrates curiosity and learning in motion. When students are encouraged to test, fail, and try again, they grow as problem-solvers and collaborators. Every mistake becomes a visible sign of growth—and that’s what makes learning truly meaningful.
